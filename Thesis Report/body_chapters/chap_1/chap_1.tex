\chapter{Introduction}

Modern chip multiprocessors (CMPs) integrate a large number of processing cores, caches, and memory components on a single die. As the number of cores increases, efficient communication between these components becomes a critical design challenge. Traditional bus-based or point-to-point interconnects do not scale well due to bandwidth limitations, contention, and wiring complexity. To address these issues, modern CMPs adopt Network-on-Chip (NoC) architectures, which organize on-chip communication as a packet-switched network composed of routers and links. This approach improves scalability, enables higher parallelism in communication, and provides structured support for routing data between distributed components. As a result, the NoC plays a central role in determining system-level performance, such as latency, throughput, and energy efficiency \cite{Dally2001}.

Figure \ref{fig:Communication_Architecture} illustrates the fundamental differences between traditional bus-based interconnects and modern NoC architectures. In a bus-based system (Fig. \ref{fig:Communication_Architecture}a), all components share a common communication medium, leading to contention and long data paths as the number of cores increases. In contrast, a NoC architecture (Fig. \ref{fig:Communication_Architecture}b) employs distributed routers and links, allowing for shorter data paths and reduced latency, even as the system scales to many cores.

While NoCs improve scalability and performance, they also introduce new reliability challenges. With continued technology scaling and increasingly demanding workloads, CMPs operate under high power densities and elevated temperatures for extended periods. These conditions accelerate device degradation mechanisms, making long-term reliability a primary concern. Two dominant aging mechanisms in modern technologies are electromigration (EM) and hot-carrier injection (HCI). Electromigration occurs in metal interconnects when sustained high current densities cause the gradual displacement of metal atoms, leading to void formation or wire thinning, and eventually open-circuit failures. In contrast, HCI affects transistors, where high-energy carriers become trapped in the gate oxide, increasing the threshold voltage and degrading transistor performance over time. Both mechanisms are strongly dependent on usage and temperature, meaning that frequently used components under high thermal stress degrade significantly faster than less utilized ones \cite{Clotho2015}. In addition to these wearout mechanisms, bias temperature instability (BTI) can also contribute to aging in transistors in the presence of prolonged bias and elevated temperatures, resulting in threshold voltage shifts and performance degradation. However, since BTI is primarily induced when circuits are underutilized, it can be mitigated through techniques such as Use it or Lose it, as proposed by Kim et al. \cite{Kim2013}. Therefore, this thesis focuses on EM and HCI as the primary aging mechanisms of concern in NoC components.

The impact of aging on NoC reliability is significant, as failures in critical components can lead to system-level failures, unlike core or cache failures that may be mitigated through redundancy or task mapping techniques. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:NoC_failure_modes}a), a core failure due to wearout may reduce computational capacity but does not necessarily lead to a complete system failure, as tasks can be remapped to other cores. In contrast, a router failure (Fig. \ref{fig:NoC_failure_modes}b) or a link failure (Fig. \ref{fig:NoC_failure_modes}c) can disrupt communication between multiple components by severing critical paths and potentially isolating parts of the network. This loss of connectivity can render multiple cores inaccessible. Consequently, the reliability of NoC components is crucial for maintaining overall system functionality and performance over time.

Routing algorithms play a critical role in determining both the performance and reliability in NoCs. By controlling how traffic is distributed across the network, routing decisions directly influence the utilization and stress experienced by routers and links. Conventional routing algorithms, such as dimension-order routing (DOR), which follows deterministic path based on a fixed order of dimensions (e.g., X-Y), do not consider the dynamic state of the network or the aging effects on components. Their deterministic nature leads to uneven traffic distribution, with central routers and links experiencing consistently higher utilization. This imbalance translates directly into accelerated aging of specific components, effectively reducing the lifetime of the entire network. To address the limitations of deterministic routing, adaptive routing algorithms have been proposed. These methods dynamically select packet routing based on network conditions, such as congestion, buffer occupancy, or link availability. By distributing traffic more evenly across the network, adaptive routing can improve both performance and load balancing \cite{Kondoth2026}. \section{Problem Statement}

Prior research on NoC reliability has largely focused on fault-tolerant routing. In these approaches, the primary objective is to maintain connectivity after faults have already occurred. Fault-tolerant routing algorithms detect failed components and reconfigure paths to avoid them, ensuring continued operation despite permanent faults \cite{Adnan2025} \cite{Samala2020} \cite{Samala2022}. However, these methods are inherently reactive: they address failures only after they manifest, and therefore do not prevent or delay the underlying aging processes that lead to these faults. In contrast, aging-aware routing adopts a proactive approach \cite{Clotho2015} \cite{AROMa2018}. Instead of reacting to failures, these algorithms aim to delay their occurrence by controlling how traffic is distributed over time. Aging-aware routing leverages metrics such as utilization, temperature, and estimated wear to guide routing decisions, with the goal of balancing stress across network components. By avoiding persistent overuse of specific routers or links, these techniques seek to extend the overall lifetime of the NoC. Like fault-tolerant methods, aging-aware routing is adaptive in nature, but its objective is fundamentally different: it optimizes long-term reliability rather than short-term fault avoidance.

Existing aging-aware routing techniques typically rely on explicit modeling of component reliability. For example, they may estimate the mean time to failure (MTTF) of links and routers based on EM and HCI models, and use these estimates to compute reliability-aware path costs. Routing decisions are then made by selecting paths that minimize accumulated degradation. While effective in principle, these approaches often require global knowledge of network state and repeated evaluation of candidate paths. This introduces significant computational complexity, memory, and footprint overhead, making them less practical for large-scale systems. Moreover, their reliance on predefined cost functions and heuristics limits their ability to adapt to dynamic and evolving traffic patterns over long execution periods \cite{Clotho2015} \cite{Bhardwaj2012}.

\section{Scope of Research}

This thesis addresses the limitations of existing aging-aware routing techniques by proposing a learning-based routing framework that dynamically adapts to both traffic conditions and progressive wear in NoC systems. Unlike prior approaches that rely on explicit fixed heuristic cost functions, the proposed method learns routing decisions directly through interaction with the network, enabling continuous adaptation without requiring global state information or repeated path evaluation. Similar to prior aging-aware routing work, the proposed approach aims to mitigate wearout by distributing traffic away from highly stressed components. To achieve this, the system monitors key stress indicators at the microarchitectural level, including switching activity and temperature, which directly influence aging mechanisms such as EM and HCI. These parameters are used in conjunction with established EM and HCI models to capture the evolving degradation state of routers and links over time.

Building on this information, the routing strategy is formulated as a reinforcement learning (RL) problem. Each router acts as an agent that learns to select output ports based on local observations of network state and stress conditions. To ensure scalability and low hardware overhead, the proposed method employs linear function approximation \cite{LARE2023}, allowing routing decisions to be computed efficiently using a compact set of features rather than maintaining large state-action tables. Through this formulation, the learned policy implicitly captures the relationship between traffic distribution and long-term aging, enabling the system to avoid overusing vulnerable components without relying on explicitly defined cost metrics.

In addition to improving adaptability, the proposed approach addresses practical implementation constraints. Since adaptive routing can introduce cyclic dependencies and deadlock, the routing algorithm is integrated with a deadlock-free design using escape virtual channels \cite{Duato1993}. This ensures correctness while preserving the flexibility of adaptive routing decisions.

The proposed routing framework is implemented within the gem5 Garnet NoC simulator, enabling cycle-accurate evaluation under realistic workloads. Experimental results demonstrate that the learned routing policy achieves improved lifetime characteristics by balancing stress across the network, while maintaining competitive performance in terms of latency and throughput.

The key contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A reinforcement learning-based aging-aware routing algorithm that dynamically adapts to traffic and wear conditions
    \item Integration of a linear function approximation model that enables scalable, low-overhead implementation at the router level
    \item Integration of microarchitectural aging indicators, including EM- and HCI-related stress factors, into the routing decision process
    \item Integration of a deadlock-free adaptive routing design using escape virtual channels
    \item Implementation and evaluation within the gem5 Garnet NoC simulator, demonstrating improved network lifetime and balanced wear distribution
\end{itemize}
